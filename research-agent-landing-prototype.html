Why this screen

  • Sell the outcome, not the mechanism. "Find your users" beats "AI agent with an MCP layer." The how (live data) earns trust later; the what wins the click now.
  • One promise that fits both audiences. The headline plus the optional link let a pre-launch founder and a shipped-but-stuck founder both see themselves.

SCREEN 2 The problem — make them feel seen resonance before pitch

Building the product was never the hard part. Finding the people who want it is.

Before you build

You've got the idea and the fear that comes with it. What if you pour in several months and nobody shows up?

After you ship

Crickets. No signups, no idea why, and every piece of advice boils down to "just post more."

Either way you're guessing. And guessing with months of your life is the most expensive thing you can do.

Why this screen

  • Agitate before you pitch. People buy when they feel understood. Naming the exact pain earns the right to offer a fix.
  • Two columns = two audiences, one page. The pre-launch fear and the post-launch silence are the two sharpest pains from your positioning. Each visitor reads their own.
  • Name the enemy. "Just post more" and "guessing" set up your wedge on the next screens: data, not guesses.
Tests

Which pain lands harder. Later you can split the page by audience and compare. For v1, watch whether people keep scrolling here or bounce.

Why this screen

  • A brand-new demand test has no testimonials, so don't fake them. Fabricated proof is a fast way to lose trust (and it's just wrong).
  • Qualify instead. "Is this me?" cards let both audiences self-select, which makes the upcoming ask feel personal.
  • An honest founder's note ("we read every submission ourselves") is real, human proof you can stand behind on day one.
Tests

Swap in over time: replace the note with real quotes, a signup counter, or logos as soon as you genuinely have them.

Why this screen

  • Only two required fields: a sentence about it, and an email. Everything else is optional, so a pre-launch founder with nothing but an idea can still sign up. That is the point of making the product link optional.
  • Field order is deliberate. Optional link first (low pressure, easy start) → optional App Store → the one required sentence → email last. People hand over an email more readily once they've already typed something.
  • "optional" is stated out loud on both link fields, so a website-only or idea-only founder never stalls wondering if it's required.
  • Trust microcopy sits at the exact point of commitment, where doubt peaks.
Tests

This is THE metric: form-start rate, field drop-off, and completed signups. Completion rate = your real demand signal.

Higher-volume variant: put a 2-field version (link + email) in the hero to catch impulse signups, and keep this full form for considered scrollers. Trade-off: hero signups arrive without context.

Why this screen

  • FAQ is objection removal, not decoration. Here the doubts are about credibility: how do you find these people, is the data current, can I trust the leads, is my idea safe. Answer them and the fence-sitters convert.
  • "How do you find them?" and "Can I trust the leads?" are the trust questions that matter most for a "we'll find your users" promise. They make the claim believable instead of magical.
  • "Do I need a finished product?" pulls double duty, reassuring the pre-launch audience one more time.
  • End on the wedge + one more CTA. The scroller who needed every doubt answered first gets their button exactly when they're finally ready.
Tests

Does objection-handling lift conversion for deep scrollers? Compare signups from the final CTA vs the hero CTA.

How to actually run this as a demand test

The page is only half the experiment. Here's the rest, kept lean.

The one number that matters

  • Signup conversion rate = completed forms ÷ unique visitors. That single number is your demand signal.
  • Rough read for a cold-traffic landing: under ~2% is weak, ~5%+ is a real "keep going," in between means iterate the hero and offer.

Where to send traffic

  • The communities your own positioning points to: founder subreddits, Indie Hackers, relevant Slack/Discord groups, X.
  • A small paid test (a few hundred dollars of search or social ads) to get clean, comparable numbers fast.
  • Your own audience and DMs, useful for signal, but weight it lower since it's warm traffic.

What to instrument before you launch

  • Scroll-depth per screen (where do people stop reading?).
  • Hero CTA clicks vs final CTA clicks.
  • Form start rate and per-field drop-off.
  • A short "what made you sign up?" question on the thank-you screen, free qualitative gold.

First things to A/B once traffic flows

  • The hero headline (three variants are on Screen 1).
  • Hero CTA scrolls-to-form vs a 2-field form inlined in the hero.
  • Pre-launch-first vs shipped-first ordering of the audience copy.
